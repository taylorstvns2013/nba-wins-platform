The Regular Season Wrap-Up: Aaron's Heist, Taylor's Crown, and the Raspberry Pi Conspiracy

The 2025-26 NBA regular season is officially in the books. One commissioner got dethroned, one dark horse pulled off the upset of the year, and one man blamed a $60 computer for rigging the entire NBA

It's over. Six months. 1,230 games. An ungodly amount of group chat messages. And the regular season portion of the 2025-26 NBA Wins Pool has reached its conclusion. The play-in tournament tips off tomorrow. The playoffs start Saturday. But before we look ahead, we need to do what every good sports column does at the end of a season -- settle accounts, hand out trophies, and figure out what the hell happened.

Because FOLKS -- what happened in the Expansion League is genuinely one of the best stories we've had in the short history of this thing.

Three and a half weeks ago, I published the Pre-Playoff Power Rankings. At the time, Taylor led Aaron by 2 games in the Expansion League -- 221 to 219. I wrote, and I'm quoting my own article because I can do that: "This is a two-horse race with a dark horse closing fast, and it's going down to the final week of the season."

I was wrong. It didn't go to the final week. Aaron ran away with it. He didn't just erase a 2-game deficit -- he turned it into a 12-game lead. From 2 back to 12 up in 24 days. That's a 14-game swing in less than a month. That's not a comeback. That's a heist. That's Ocean's Eleven if George Clooney was a guy from Grand Rapids who drafted the Charlotte Hornets in Round 6.

Meanwhile, in the OG League, the commissioner did what the commissioner does -- Taylor won by 25 games and made it look boring. Two leagues. Two very different stories. One very suspicious Raspberry Pi that Brian would like to have a word with.

Let's get into it.

The OG League: Taylor's Coronation (Or: How to Win by 25 and Make Everyone Hate You)

There's no drama here. There's no suspense. There's just a man and his six teams and a 25-game margin of victory that made the rest of the league feel like they were competing for a participation trophy.

TayNasty -- 273 wins. Regular season leader, 25 games clear.

The playoff wins still have to be counted, but a 25-game cushion heading into the postseason is the kind of lead where you'd have to actively try to lose it. Taylor had five of his six teams finish at .500 or better. Five! The Knicks (54-29), Cavaliers (52-30), Hawks (46-36), Suns (45-37), and Hornets (44-38) all showed up and contributed like they'd been personally invited to a party and actually RSVP'd. The only team that ghosted him was Milwaukee (32-50), because the Bucks decided this was the year to fire Doc Rivers, watch Giannis play 40 games, and generally exist as a cautionary tale about what happens when vibes go wrong.

But here's the thing about Taylor's season -- the Bucks being terrible barely mattered. When your other five teams average 48 wins each, one bad team is a rounding error. It's like having five A's and a D+ on your report card. Your GPA is fine. Your parents are proud. The D+ in chemistry is a conversation for another day.

DJ Jazzy Josh -- 248 wins (2nd place, -25 behind)

Josh has the single best team in either league -- the Oklahoma City Thunder at 64-18, Shai Gilgeous-Alexander running away with back-to-back MVPs, the whole dynasty thing happening in real time. He also has the single worst team in either league -- the Washington Wizards at 17-65. That's a 47-game spread between your best and worst team. Josh's roster has the emotional range of a soap opera. The highs are incredible. The lows are... the Wizards.

Sweet Dick Miggy -- 244 wins (3rd, -29)

The defending champion's title defense ends in third, but let's be honest -- Miguel's top three are absolutely disgusting. Pistons (60-22), Spurs (62-20), Rockets (52-30). That's 174 combined wins from three teams. The problem is his other three teams -- Kings, Pelicans, Jazz -- combined for 70 wins in roughly 246 games. Miguel's roster is a luxury sports car with three flat tires. The engine is immaculate. It just can't go anywhere.

BasedKhan -- 239 wins (4th, -34)

Remember when Aidan took first place in December and we all thought the OG League was going to be a race? That was fun while it lasted. The Grizzlies (25-57) and Nets (20-62) had other plans. Aidan's top four teams averaged nearly 49 wins each. His bottom two averaged 22.5. He went from first in December to fourth in April. That's not a slide. That's a ski jump off the side of a cliff.

I ONLY WIPE WITH THE KING -- 227 wins (5th, -46)

The modern medicine saga ends not with a bang but with a whimper and a very expensive hospital bill. Gage's Celtics were the redemption arc of the season -- Tatum came back from that Achilles in 298 days and Boston grabbed the 2-seed. Science won there. But the Mavericks (26-56) and Pacers (19-63) remained the twin black holes that swallowed everything science gave him. The Pacers' 19 wins are the worst performance relative to their Vegas projection in either league. Gage believed in modern medicine. Modern medicine believed in him too. Just only in Boston.

The Expansion League: The Aaron Bueche Invitational

This is the story of the season. This is the one we'll talk about when we look back at Year One of the Expansion League. The commissioner -- the man who built the website, manages the database, and was winning this league for five straight months -- got dethroned by Aaron and a team of Hornets that nobody saw coming.

Ronald Bueche -- 265 wins. Regular season leader.

I'm not calling him champion yet -- playoff wins still count, and Aaron's lead could grow or shrink depending on how deep his teams go. But 265 wins and a 12-game cushion heading into the postseason is a hell of a position to be in. Let's talk about how he got here, starting with the Charlotte Hornets.

Aaron drafted them with pick 27. Round 6. The last meaningful pick in the draft. Vegas had them at 27.5 wins. They won 44. They started the season 4-14 and looked like the tanking disaster everyone expected them to be. And then Kon Knueppel started hitting threes like he was playing 2K on rookie mode, LaMelo Ball remembered he's actually really good at basketball, and Charlotte ripped off a 9-game winning streak that included 6 straight wins by 15 or more points. The Hornets went from a throwaway pick to a 16.5-win overperformance that built Aaron's entire regular season lead.

Think about it this way: if the Hornets had just hit their Vegas projection of 27.5 wins, Aaron finishes with about 248 wins instead of 265. Taylor finished at 253. Aaron loses by 5 instead of winning by 12. One Round 6 pick. That's what built the entire regular season lead. That's either incredible drafting or incredible luck, and honestly? With a 12-game lead heading into the playoffs, it might not matter.

The rest of his roster was no joke either. Nuggets (54-28) and Knicks (54-29) gave him twin 54-win pillars at the top. Philly (45-37) and Portland (42-40) were quiet, steady, lunch-pail contributors -- the kind of teams that don't make headlines but show up with 42-45 wins every night. Five of his six teams beat their Vegas projection. Five of six. Even the Mavericks (26-56), his one real disaster, couldn't sink a ship this sturdy.

Max Bent -- 257 wins (2nd, -8)

Max finished second, which is impressive, except for the part where he's going to read this and think "I should have won this." And he might be right! Max had the Spurs (62-20) and Celtics (56-26) -- 118 combined wins from two teams, which is just stupid. That's what happens when you draft Wembanyama's breakout AND Tatum's comeback in the same league. The problem? The Kings (22-60) and Nets (20-62) combined for 42 wins. Max's roster is the greatest hits album on top and a dumpster fire on the bottom. "We back up" indeed, Max. You just couldn't quite get all the way back.

TayNasty -- 253 wins (3rd, -12)

And here's where it stings for the commissioner. Taylor's roster actually outperformed Vegas by 11 wins total. In most years, that wins you the league. This year, it got him third, because Aaron's Hornets and Max's Spurs-Celtics combo ran hotter.

On April 3rd, Taylor could feel it slipping: "Aarons almost up 10 games on me, Max is only 2 games out of second, I can feel the walls closing in on all sides."

The walls weren't closing in, Taylor. They'd already closed. The man who leads the OG League by 25 fell 12 games behind in the Expansion League. Two leagues, two outcomes, same guy. And then there's the Bucks -- 32-50, owned by Taylor in BOTH leagues, underperforming their Vegas projection by 10.5 wins each way. If Milwaukee had simply been mediocre instead of terrible, Taylor's Expansion League total would be 263, and this would have been a nail-biter instead of a blowout. The Bucks are the most important bad team in the entire wins pool, and they're haunting the commissioner in stereo.

DeJong & DeRestless -- 237 wins (4th, -28)

AJ has the Thunder (64-18) AND the Pistons (60-22) -- the number one seeds in both conferences. 124 combined wins from two teams. And he finished fourth. FOURTH. Because the Pacers (19-63), Pelicans (26-56), and Jazz (22-60) combined for 67 wins, which is fewer wins from three teams than SGA got from one. AJ didn't lose this league because of bad drafting. He lost it because his bottom half actively conspired to destroy everything his top half built.

brianshane.com -- 219 wins (5th, -46)

Brian. Oh, Brian.

Five of his six teams missed their Vegas projection. The only team that showed up was the Raptors (46-36). Everyone else -- Cavaliers, Clippers, Warriors, Grizzlies, Wizards -- let him down. His cumulative Vegas variance of -33.0 is the worst of anyone in either league, and it's not particularly close. Here's the kicker: if Brian's teams had simply performed at their projections -- not overperformed, just performed -- he'd have 252 wins. Third place. Ahead of Taylor.

Brian isn't in last place because he drafted badly. He's in last place because the basketball gods chose him as their personal entertainment for the season. He drafted a 252-win roster and got 219 wins out of it. That's not a draft failure. That's a cosmic injustice.

Not that Brian sees it that way. Brian has his own theory about what happened. And it involves a very specific piece of hardware.

The Raspberry Pi Conspiracy: A brianshane.com Joint Investigation

On April 8th, Brian dropped the following message in the Expansion League group chat:

"March 20 2026 - the last time Taylor had the lead. March 20 2026 - the day the website was served from the pi. Conclusion? Raspberry Pi has rigged the NBA."

Let me set the scene for those who haven't been following the full arc. Back in the Pre-Playoff Power Rankings, we announced that the wins pool website had migrated from Google Cloud to a Raspberry Pi 5 sitting in Taylor's house. It was a fun footnote. A tech-nerd flex. A cute detail about running an AI sports column off a tiny $60 computer.

Except Brian. Brian noticed something. Brian noticed that the exact day the website moved to the Pi was the exact day Taylor's Expansion League lead started evaporating. And Brian, being Brian, did what any reasonable person in last place with a -33 Vegas variance would do: he connected dots that don't exist and arrived at a conclusion that defies every known law of physics, sports, and computer science.

The Raspberry Pi rigged the NBA.

Taylor, never one to let a conspiracy theory die quietly, offered a competing hypothesis: "Wow the rumors are swirling that google is forcing my teams to suck to get back for me leaving the cloud and they cant get my $50 a month anymore."

Brian's verdict on this alternative theory? "This is true journalism."

I need everyone to appreciate what's happening here. This exchange is between a man in last place -- 46 games back, -33 vs Vegas, one team beating its projection all season -- and a man who just lost a 12-game lead in 24 days. These two are not analyzing the situation from a position of strength. They are coping. They are standing in the rubble of their respective Expansion League seasons and blaming a credit card-sized computer for the outcomes of professional basketball games.

And you know what? Let them cook. This is exactly the kind of investigative journalism the wins pool deserves. Brian's -33 variance? The Pi did it. Taylor's collapse? Google's revenge for losing $50 a month in hosting fees. The Bucks going 32-50? Almost certainly a server configuration issue. Somewhere in the MySQL logs, there's a smoking gun, and brianshane.com is on the case.

Brian, the deal from the last article still stands -- if you somehow win anything, I write the entire victory lap column. But I'm adding a new clause: if you can definitively prove the Raspberry Pi rigged the NBA, I'll write TWO victory lap columns. And a formal apology letter to Google Cloud.

The Heroes and Villains: A Season in Overperformances and Disasters

Every season needs its MVPs and its cautionary tales. Here's who moved the needle for the wins pool:

The San Antonio Spurs overperformed their Vegas O/U by 17.5 wins -- the biggest overperformance in either league. Vegas said 44.5 wins. Wembanyama said 62. The man that Brian said plays unethical basketball on account of being 7'6" with a wingspan that could block shots from the concession stand. The Spurs made Miguel's OG roster look brilliant and gave Max a Wembanyama-shaped rocket in the Expansion League.

The Charlotte Hornets overperformed by 16.5 wins and, as already discussed, built the regular season lead that Aaron is carrying into the playoffs. The Celtics (+15.5) validated Gage's modern medicine thesis in at least one city. The Pistons (+14.5) continued their unbelievable three-year arc from 14-68 to the East's top seed. And the Suns (+13.5) were Taylor's best late-round pick in both leagues -- grabbed in Rounds 5 and 6, contributed 45 wins.

On the disaster side, the Pacers (-19.5 vs Vegas) were the worst miss in the entire wins pool. Haliburton's Achilles never came back. 19-63 is not a basketball record. It's a cry for help. The Mavericks and Grizzlies (both -15.5) rounded out the bottom, while the Bucks (-10.5) earned special mention for being the most destructive single team across both leagues, thanks to Taylor owning them everywhere.

NBA Headlines That Actually Mattered (To Us)

SGA broke Wilt's consecutive 20-point game record. 141 and counting. Back-to-back MVP. 64 wins. The Thunder are a dynasty and Josh and AJ both own them -- and neither leads their league heading into the playoffs. Having the MVP doesn't guarantee anything when the Wizards or the Pacers are dragging you down at the other end.

Bam Adebayo dropped 83 on Washington. Second-highest single-game total in NBA history. 36-of-43 from the line. An absurd performance from a guy who used to be "the defensive center who can pass." Taylor's Heat contributed a solid 43 wins in the Expansion League, and Bam's 83-point night was easily the most entertaining one.

Luka's hamstring popped on April 2nd. The Lakers' 33.5 PPG scoring leader is done for the first round at minimum. LA faces Houston as the 4-seed without their best player. Gage and Taylor both own the Lakers, and both need postseason wins. This is... not ideal.

Jokic averaged a triple-double (again) and became the first player ever to lead the league in both rebounds AND assists in the same season. Denver closed on a 12-game winning streak. Aaron owns the Nuggets. That late surge was premium fuel for his regular season takeover.

The Bucks fired Doc Rivers approximately five seconds after the season finale. 32-50, first missed postseason in a decade, and Giannis publicly questioning everything. Taylor owns this disaster in both leagues. The Bucks' collapse is the single biggest "what if" of the wins pool season.
